How I Overcame My Fear of Speaking English in Front of Others

Personal reflection • Reading mode — Dark

For many years, speaking English in front of others felt like a huge mountain I was not prepared to climb.

I always worried about making mistakes, pronouncing something wrong, or simply forgetting what I wanted to say. Even though I studied and practiced on my own, the moment I had to use English in front of people, my mind would go blank. This fear became a wall that stopped my progress and made me doubt my ability to truly learn the language.

The turning point came when I realized that fear itself was teaching me something. Every time I avoided speaking, I felt disappointed in myself. One day, during a group activity, I decided that avoiding the challenge was no longer an option. I told myself that even if I made mistakes, it was better to try than to stay silent. That small decision changed everything. It wasn’t about being perfect; it was about being brave enough to begin.

My first step was preparation. I started practicing short sentences in front of a mirror, recording myself, and even talking alone while doing my daily tasks. At first, it felt strange, but soon I noticed improvement. Preparing phrases and possible answers reduced my anxiety because I felt more in control of what I wanted to express. Little by little, I gained familiarity with the sound of my own voice speaking English.

The second step was practice with real people. I joined small study groups and participated in simple conversations. At first, my voice trembled and my hands shook, but every time I spoke, I realized something important: people were not judging me. Most of them were learning too, and they understood how difficult it can be. Instead of criticism, I received support, corrections, and encouragement. This made me feel more confident and motivated to continue.

“The real transformation happened when I dared to speak English in front of a larger audience.”

The real transformation happened when I dared to speak English in front of a larger audience. My biggest presentation was when my team and I introduced a digital project we had developed: TrackMate, an automotive maintenance application designed to help users track services, repairs, and vehicle information. It was a project we had worked on for weeks, and presenting it in English felt intimidating. I prepared my part of the presentation over and over again, practicing the explanation of the app’s features, its purpose, and why it was useful.

When the day arrived, I felt the familiar fear rising inside me. But I reminded myself of all the preparation I had done. I took a deep breath and began my part of the presentation. My voice was not perfect, and I stumbled over a few words, but I delivered the message clearly. When I finished, I looked up and saw my classmates and teachers listening attentively and nodding. Their reaction filled me with relief and pride. It was then that I understood how much I had grown.

Looking back, I can say that this experience marked a turning point in my learning. I realized that fear can turn into confidence through practice, preparation, and courage. Speaking English is not just about vocabulary or grammar; it is about believing in your ability to improve. Every attempt counts, and every mistake teaches you something valuable.

Today, I no longer avoid situations where I have to speak English. Instead, I see them as opportunities to grow. The fear is still there sometimes, but it no longer controls me. If I had waited to feel completely ready, I would still be silent. By daring to take that step, I discovered a stronger, braver version of myself.

And that is the greatest lesson I learned: confidence is not something you magically get — it is something you build, one word at a time.
